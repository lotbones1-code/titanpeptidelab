Delivery Science

What are peptide nasal sprays and why nasal delivery?

Peptides are short chains of amino acids — typically 2 to 50 residues — that act as signaling molecules throughout the body. Unlike small-molecule drugs, they are substrate-specific, meaning they interact with defined receptor families rather than broadly affecting multiple organ systems. This specificity is why the research interest in peptides has compounded over the past two decades.

A nasal spray is a sterile aqueous or buffered suspension delivered via a calibrated pump actuator directly into the nasal cavity. Each actuation delivers a defined volume (typically 100–200 μL) and, therefore, a defined peptide mass per dose.

The nasal mucosa as a delivery site

The nasal cavity offers three physiologically distinct pathways for peptide absorption:

  • Systemic absorption via the nasal mucosa. The highly vascularised respiratory epithelium allows polar molecules to cross directly into the bloodstream, bypassing hepatic first-pass metabolism. This is the primary pathway for most peptides in the catalog.
  • Olfactory nerve pathway. The olfactory epithelium in the upper nasal vault sits in direct anatomical proximity to the cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone. Molecules transported along olfactory axons can reach the olfactory bulb and, from there, distribute into the cerebrospinal fluid and central nervous system. This pathway is believed to contribute to the rapid CNS effects observed with neuropeptides like Semax and Selank at doses too low to explain through systemic distribution alone.
  • Trigeminal nerve pathway. A secondary neural conduit that also terminates in the brainstem. Relevant primarily for CNS-targeted compounds.

Why this matters practically: CNS-active peptides (Semax, Selank, DSIP) may reach relevant brain concentrations faster via nasal administration than via subcutaneous injection, where systemic distribution precedes any blood-brain barrier crossing. The olfactory pathway bypasses the BBB entirely.

Formulation requirements

A quality peptide nasal spray requires:

  • Sterile water for injection (WFI) or isotonic saline as the primary vehicle. Tap water or non-sterile solvents introduce endotoxin risk.
  • Buffering agents (typically acetate or phosphate buffer) to maintain pH 4.5–6.5, which protects peptide stability and minimises mucosal irritation.
  • Preservative (benzalkonium chloride at 0.01–0.02% is most common) to prevent microbial growth in multi-dose containers after opening.
  • Calibrated pump actuator with a defined per-spray volume. Titan Peptide Lab sprays use a 100 μL actuator, making dose calculation straightforward.

Pharmacokinetics

Bioavailability comparison: nasal vs injection vs oral

Bioavailability (BA) is the fraction of an administered dose that reaches systemic circulation unchanged. A subcutaneous injection of a stable peptide typically achieves near-100% BA (the compound goes directly into interstitial fluid and lymphatics). Every other route must be evaluated against that baseline.

For peptides, the oral route is almost always non-viable as a research delivery method. The gastrointestinal tract destroys most peptides within minutes through acid hydrolysis and protease activity. Even with enteric coatings, systemic BA for most peptides is below 5%.

Nasal bioavailability: the honest picture

Nasal BA for peptides is compound-dependent and ranges widely. The literature reports:

Subcutaneous injection (reference) ~100%
Nasal — small peptides (<1 kDa) 30–70%
Nasal — medium peptides (1–4 kDa) 10–40%
Oral peptides (unprotected) <5%

The key variables that determine where a specific compound lands in that range are: molecular weight, lipophilicity, mucosal permeability enhancers in the formulation, residence time in the nasal cavity (influenced by mucociliary clearance, which takes 15–20 minutes), and whether absorption occurs via the olfactory or respiratory epithelium.

Why injection BA is not always the goal

For CNS-targeted neuropeptides, systemic BA may be secondary to CNS BA. A compound delivered intranasally that reaches the olfactory bulb via the olfactory nerve may produce CNS concentrations at a lower effective systemic dose than subcutaneous injection, which requires BBB crossing. This has been proposed as the mechanism behind Semax’s apparent dose-efficiency advantage in nasal format.

Dose equivalency: how to think about it

Because nasal BA is lower than subcutaneous for most peptides, researchers typically use higher nasal doses to achieve equivalent systemic exposure. A rough heuristic for preliminary research planning:

Compound Nasal BA estimate Approximate nasal:SC dose ratio
Semax (heptapeptide, ~820 Da) 30–50% 2–3×
Selank (heptapeptide, ~751 Da) 30–50% 2–3×
BPC-157 (pentadecapeptide, ~1.4 kDa) 10–30% 3–5×
PT-141 (bremelanotide, ~1.0 kDa) 30–60% 1.5–3×
Oxytocin (nonapeptide, ~1.0 kDa) 20–40% 2.5–5×
DSIP (nonapeptide, ~850 Da) 20–40% 2.5–5×

These are estimates for research planning only. Pharmacokinetic data in humans is limited for most of these compounds. Actual bioavailability varies significantly with formulation, administration technique, nasal physiology, and congestion status.

Compound profiles

The following profiles cover what the peer-reviewed literature says, the proposed mechanisms, and typical dose ranges used in existing research. They are written for researchers, not consumers. None of the compounds below are approved pharmaceutical agents in most jurisdictions.

BPC-157 — Body Protective Compound Pentadecapeptide
Sequence length
15 amino acids
Molecular weight
~1,419 Da
Research dose range
200–600 μg/day
Overview

BPC-157 is a 15-residue peptide fragment derived from a portion of human gastric juice protein. It does not exist as a natural circulating peptide but is a synthetic analog of a gastric protective protein sequence. It has been extensively studied in rodent models for its effects on tendon, ligament, bone, and gastrointestinal healing. In vitro, it upregulates growth hormone receptor expression and modulates nitric oxide signaling.

Key research areas

The majority of published BPC-157 research uses intraperitoneal or oral administration in rodents. The compound consistently accelerates healing of surgically induced tendon and ligament injuries (Achilles, cruciate, rotator cuff models), reduces NSAID-induced gastric lesions, attenuates alcohol-induced gastric and liver damage, and improves anastomotic healing. Several studies have also documented effects on dopaminergic and serotonergic systems.

Nasal-specific notes

As a larger peptide (1.4 kDa), BPC-157 has lower nasal bioavailability than the smaller neuropeptides in this catalog. Nasal doses in research contexts are typically 2–4× the injectable reference dose. The compound has no clinically significant nasal mucosal irritation profile in existing data.

Typical research dose range

Animal studies use 1–10 μg/kg (intraperitoneal or oral). Extrapolating to 70 kg: ~70–700 μg systemic. Nasal research protocols typically use 200–600 μg/day split across 1–2 administrations.

Selank — Anxiolytic Neuropeptide Heptapeptide
Sequence
Thr-Lys-Pro-Arg-Pro-Gly-Pro
Molecular weight
~751 Da
Research dose range
250–1,000 μg/day
Overview

Selank is a synthetic analog of the endogenous neuropeptide tuftsin (Thr-Lys-Pro-Arg), with a C-terminal Pro-Gly-Pro extension added to increase metabolic stability. It was developed at the Institute of Molecular Genetics of the Russian Academy of Sciences and approved in Russia and Ukraine as a registered medicine for generalised anxiety. This makes it one of the few compounds in this catalog with a formal regulatory approval — albeit outside Western jurisdictions.

Key research areas

Selank’s anxiolytic profile is well-documented: it reduces anxiety-like behavior in rodent models (elevated plus maze, open field) without inducing sedation, motor impairment, or the withdrawal profile of benzodiazepines. It upregulates expression of BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) and modulates IL-6 and TNF-α levels, suggesting an anti-inflammatory CNS component. Memory and learning studies in rodents show improved acquisition without cognitive impairment.

Nasal delivery advantage

As a small heptapeptide, Selank has relatively favorable nasal bioavailability. Its CNS effects are hypothesised to involve the olfactory nerve pathway, which may explain its unusually rapid onset (often observed within 10–20 minutes in research subjects) at doses that seem low by systemic standards. Nasal administration is the approved route in Russian clinical practice.

Typical research dose range

Clinical data from Russia uses 250–500 μg per nostril, twice daily (total 500–1,000 μg/day) over 5–14 day periods. Longer-term protocols use 5 days on, 2 days off.

Semax — Cognitive Neuropeptide Heptapeptide
Sequence
Met-Glu-His-Phe-Pro-Gly-Pro
Molecular weight
~820 Da
Research dose range
200–600 μg/day
Overview

Semax is a synthetic heptapeptide derived from the ACTH(4-10) fragment, modified with a Pro-Gly-Pro C-terminal extension to prevent rapid enzymatic degradation. Like Selank, it is an approved pharmaceutical in Russia, where it has been used clinically for stroke rehabilitation, attention deficit, and optic nerve atrophy. It is among the most-studied synthetic neuropeptides in the Russian pharmacological literature, with over 100 publications.

Key research areas

Semax’s primary mechanism involves rapid BDNF and NGF (nerve growth factor) upregulation in the hippocampus and cortex. In stroke models, it significantly reduces infarct area when administered within the first 24 hours. Cognitive studies show improved working memory, attention, and executive function in both rodent and human subjects. It also potentiates dopamine and serotonin transmission in the prefrontal cortex. Unlike stimulants, it does not produce peripheral sympathomimetic effects.

Typical research dose range

Russian clinical protocols use 200–400 μg twice daily intranasally over 5–14 day cycles. Acute cognitive protocols use single 200–600 μg doses. Higher doses (1.5 mg/day) have been used in stroke research without significant adverse events.

PT-141 — Bremelanotide Cyclic heptapeptide
Parent compound
MT-II analog
Molecular weight
~1,025 Da
Research dose range
0.5–2.0 mg
Overview

PT-141 (bremelanotide) is a cyclic heptapeptide derived from Melanotan II. It acts as a non-selective melanocortin receptor agonist, with particular affinity for MC3-R and MC4-R. Unlike PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil), PT-141 works via CNS melanocortin signaling in the hypothalamus rather than through peripheral vasodilation. FDA-approved bremelanotide (Vyleesi) is an injectable formulation for HSDD; this establishes significant clinical research precedent.

Key research areas

The compound’s primary research area is sexual function research — both arousal (MC3-R) and erectile function (MC4-R in the hypothalamic PVN). Clinical trials reported approximately 50% of subjects achieving the primary arousal endpoint vs 20% placebo. Additional melanocortin research covers its melanogenesis effects (tanning at higher doses, from its MT-II lineage), nausea as a dose-dependent side effect at >1.5 mg, and transient blood pressure elevation.

Nasal vs subcutaneous

The original clinical development of PT-141 used nasal delivery. The nasal formulation was eventually deprioritized due to dose variability from nasal congestion; the FDA-approved product is subcutaneous. Research nasal doses are typically 0.5–2 mg with onset at 30–90 minutes.

Oxytocin — Bonding Neuropeptide Nonapeptide
Origin
Endogenous / hypothalamic
Molecular weight
~1,007 Da
Research dose range
16–40 IU intranasal
Overview

Oxytocin is an endogenous nonapeptide produced in the paraventricular and supraoptic nuclei of the hypothalamus and released from the posterior pituitary. It is one of the most extensively researched neuropeptides in existence, with >5,000 publications. Intranasal oxytocin has been used as a research tool in hundreds of human studies, making its pharmacological profile unusually well-characterised for a peptide nasal spray.

Key research areas

Intranasal oxytocin research covers: social cognition and face recognition, trust and prosocial behavior (the Fehr trust game paradigm), autism spectrum disorder (social communication, a major active research area with multiple clinical trials), anxiety reduction, fear extinction, bonding behavior, and maternal behavior. Effects are strongly context-dependent — the same dose can increase anxiety in threatening social contexts while decreasing it in safe ones (the “social salience” hypothesis).

Typical research dose

The vast majority of human research uses 24–40 IU intranasally. Peptide mass equivalent: 24 IU ≈ 50 μg. Half-life in plasma is 3–5 minutes; CNS effects via olfactory nerve pathway persist longer. Dosing studies suggest effects peak at 45–75 minutes post-administration.

DSIP — Delta Sleep-Inducing Peptide Nonapeptide
Sequence
Trp-Ala-Gly-Gly-Asp-Ala-Ser-Gly-Glu
Molecular weight
~850 Da
Research dose range
100–600 μg
Overview

DSIP was first isolated from rabbit cerebral venous blood during natural sleep by Monnier and colleagues in 1977. It is a naturally occurring nonapeptide found in the hypothalamus, pituitary, and peripheral tissues. Its name reflects the original observation that intraventricular injection in rabbits induced delta-wave (slow-wave) sleep. It is one of the few sleep-related compounds with a biological source and endogenous identity.

Key research areas

DSIP research covers: delta sleep induction and slow-wave sleep architecture, stress adaptation (attenuates ACTH and cortisol responses to chronic stress in rodent models), circadian rhythm normalisation, antioxidant activity (direct radical scavenging has been documented in vitro), and anticonvulsant effects. The stress-normalization data is arguably more robust than the sleep data — several studies show DSIP reducing HPA axis dysregulation without direct sedation.

Typical research dose range

Animal studies use 30–300 μg/kg IV or IP. Human case reports and small studies reference 100–600 μg. Nasal delivery requires higher doses due to bioavailability. Research protocols typically administer 30–60 minutes before target sleep onset.

Quality Control

How to read a Certificate of Analysis (COA)

A Certificate of Analysis is the primary document that certifies the identity, purity, and quality of a peptide batch. Knowing how to read one — and how to spot when one has been fabricated or padded — is essential due diligence for any researcher.

What a valid COA must contain

  • Compound name and CAS number. The COA should state the full IUPAC name or standard nomenclature, plus a CAS registry number if applicable. A COA that only says “BPC-157” without structural identification is insufficient.
  • Lot number. Every batch must have a unique lot number that links the COA to the physical bottle you received. Ask your supplier: “Does the lot number on my label match a COA on your website?” If they cannot provide this linkage, walk away.
  • HPLC purity result with method stated. High-Performance Liquid Chromatography is the gold standard for peptide purity. The COA should state the HPLC method (reverse-phase C18 is standard), the column and gradient conditions, and the result as a percentage area. Anything below 98% should be questioned; Titan Peptide Lab targets ≥99%.
  • Mass spectrometry (MS) confirmation. HPLC confirms purity but not identity. Mass spectrometry confirms that the dominant compound is the correct molecule with the expected molecular weight. Look for [M+H]¹¹ or [M+2H]²¹ peaks matching the theoretical mass of the peptide.
  • Testing laboratory name and accreditation. Ideally, the testing lab is ISO 17025 accredited and independent from the manufacturer. First-party COAs (manufacturer testing their own product) are not inherently invalid but carry a conflict-of-interest caveat.
  • Test date. Purity values degrade. A COA dated 2019 for a product shipped in 2026 tells you nothing about current purity. Look for dates within 12 months of your order.
  • Moisture content. Lyophilised (freeze-dried) peptides absorb water. High moisture content means your peptide mass per unit weight is lower than stated. Look for moisture <5% by Karl Fischer or similar method.
Red flags — when to reject a COA
  • No lot number, or lot number doesn’t match your product label
  • HPLC result given without method, column, or gradient details
  • Purity shown as a single number with no chromatogram trace or retention time
  • No mass spectrometry data — you cannot confirm identity without it
  • COA on company letterhead only, no independent lab name
  • Generic COA downloadable from the product page (same document for all lot numbers)
  • Test date more than 18 months old for a multi-dose liquid formulation
  • Purity stated as “≥98%” without the actual measured value
  • No endotoxin test for injectable or nasal formulations

The endotoxin question

Bacterial endotoxins (lipopolysaccharides from gram-negative bacteria) are not detectable by HPLC or mass spectrometry — they are not peptides. For nasal formulations, endotoxin contamination can cause local inflammation, epistaxis, and systemic inflammatory responses. A complete COA for a nasal spray should include an endotoxin test result (<0.1 EU/mL for most applications). If yours does not, ask the supplier for it specifically.

Handling Protocol

Storage and handling best practices

Peptides are relatively fragile molecules. Incorrect storage is one of the primary causes of failed research outcomes and wasted product. The following guidance applies to both lyophilised powder and reconstituted nasal spray formulations.

❄

Temperature

Unopened nasal sprays: refrigerate at 2–8°C (standard household fridge). Do not freeze completed formulations. Lyophilised powder can be stored at −20°C for long-term archiving.

🌄

Light exposure

Most peptides are photosensitive. Store in amber glass or opaque containers. Keep in a dark refrigerator drawer. UV exposure degrades Met-containing peptides (Semax) particularly rapidly.

💧

Humidity

Lyophilised peptides are hygroscopic. Store in sealed vials with desiccant. Do not open cold vials until they have reached room temperature (prevent condensation into the powder).

⚙

Pump handling

Recap after every use. Do not shake vigorously. Prime the pump (2–3 actuations to air) before first use to ensure consistent spray volume. Replace caps between uses.

Opened spray stability

Compound Opened stability at 4°C Key stability consideration
BPC-157 4–6 weeks Relatively stable; methionine-free
Selank 3–5 weeks Disulfide-free; good aqueous stability
Semax 3–4 weeks Methionine residue — light-sensitive
PT-141 3–5 weeks Cyclic structure improves stability
Oxytocin 3–4 weeks Disulfide bond — avoid reducing agents
DSIP 3–5 weeks No disulfide; stable in neutral pH

General rule: If you observe cloudiness, precipitate, or colour change in a previously clear solution, discard the product. Peptide degradation does not always produce visible changes, but visible changes always indicate degradation.

Shipping and cold-chain

Peptide nasal sprays should not exceed 25°C for extended periods. For short-term shipping (1–3 days), room temperature exposure is acceptable. International shipments that may sit in customs for days should be sent with gel ice or dry ice packs where permitted. Research Protocols

Stacking protocols

Peptide stacks combine two or more compounds with complementary or synergistic mechanisms. The following protocols are derived from the research literature and researcher community data. All are for laboratory investigation purposes.

Important: Combining compounds multiplies complexity. Establish individual compound baselines before combining. Document all variables. The protocols below represent what the existing literature supports — they are starting points for investigation, not prescriptions.

Cognitive Enhancement Stack Semax + Selank

Rationale: Semax upregulates BDNF/NGF and enhances dopaminergic signaling for attention and executive function. Selank provides anxiolytic action and BDNF support with a calmer activation profile. Together they address both the stimulating and stabilising components of cognitive performance research.

Protocol: Semax 200–400 μg AM (cognitive focus window). Selank 250–500 μg AM alongside Semax or separately mid-afternoon (anxiety/mood). Some researchers prefer Semax-only mornings with Selank afternoon to separate profiles.

Cycle: 5 days on, 2 days off. 4–6 weeks per research period. 2-week washout between extended cycles. This stack is the most-studied combination in the Russian neuropeptide literature.

BDNF stack Focus + calm Neuroprotective
Recovery & Repair Stack BPC-157 + DSIP

Rationale: BPC-157 addresses peripheral tissue healing (musculoskeletal, GI, vascular) while DSIP supports sleep architecture during which repair processes are most active. DSIP also reduces HPA axis stress that can impair healing cascades.

Protocol: BPC-157 250–500 μg AM (takes advantage of morning GHRH peaks). DSIP 200–400 μg approximately 60 minutes before target sleep time. Do not co-administer — separate by at least 8 hours.

Cycle: BPC-157 can run continuously for 4–8 weeks in healing research. DSIP typically cycles 5 days on, 2 days off or acute dosing only. Monitor sleep quality objectively where possible (wearable SWS tracking).

Social Behaviour Research Stack Oxytocin + Selank

Rationale: Oxytocin modulates prosocial behavior and trust, while Selank reduces baseline anxiety that may confound social performance research. Selank also reduces inflammatory signaling that has been observed to interfere with oxytocin receptor sensitivity in stressed models.

Protocol: Selank 250–500 μg 20–30 minutes prior to social/research session. Oxytocin 24 IU 30–45 minutes prior to the session (effects peak at 45–75 min). The timing gap prevents simultaneous peak overlap if that is not the research intent.

Compounds that should not be combined

  • PT-141 + Oxytocin: Both influence sexual arousal pathways via different mechanisms. The interaction is poorly characterised; simultaneous use is not supported by existing literature.
  • Multiple CNS stimulating compounds simultaneously: Semax produces stimulating effects. Adding other excitatory compounds without established baselines introduces confounding variables and unknown risk.
  • BPC-157 + peptides with known gastric effects: BPC-157 directly modulates gastric physiology. Co-administering compounds with GI side effects (nausea, PT-141 at higher doses) may produce confounded observations.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

How do I prime the nasal spray before first use?
Hold the bottle upright, point away from face, and actuate 3–5 times until a fine mist appears. This fills the pump mechanism with liquid and ensures the first research dose delivers the correct volume (not air). Re-prime after any period of non-use longer than 72 hours.
What is the correct administration technique?
Gently blow the nose to clear the nasal passages. Tilt the head slightly forward (not back — you want the solution to contact the upper nasal mucosa, not drain immediately to the throat). Insert the nozzle just inside one nostril, close the other nostril lightly, and actuate while inhaling gently. Breathe in slowly for 5–10 seconds. Repeat in the other nostril if the dose protocol requires it. Do not sniff forcefully.
How do I know how much peptide each spray delivers?
Each Titan Peptide Lab spray uses a 100 μL per actuation pump. The peptide concentration is stated on the bottle label (e.g., “5 mg/mL” BPC-157 = 500 μg per 100 μL actuation). Multiply concentration by 0.1 mL to get micrograms per spray. The total bottle volume divided by 0.1 mL gives the number of actuations available.
Why does nasal spray sometimes drip down the throat?
This is normal, especially with larger volumes. The nasal mucosa has limited absorption capacity per unit time. Mucociliary clearance moves excess fluid to the nasopharynx within minutes. To minimise this: use the head-forward position, reduce per-actuation volume by splitting doses across both nostrils, and avoid immediately blowing the nose after administration (allow 2–3 minutes for absorption).
Can I store reconstituted sprays in the freezer?
Not recommended for fully formulated nasal sprays. Freezing can disrupt the preservative system and damage the pump mechanism. Lyophilised powder can be stored at −20°C before reconstitution. Once formulated, keep at 2–8°C and use within the stated opened stability window.
What does it mean when a peptide is “acetate salt” vs “TFA salt”?
During HPLC purification, peptides are typically eluted using trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), which binds as a counter-ion to the peptide. TFA is cytotoxic at research concentrations. A properly prepared nasal spray should use acetate-exchanged peptide — the counter-ion is replaced with acetic acid during a second purification step. Always confirm your supplier uses acetate salt for nasal formulations, not the raw TFA salt from synthesis.
Is there any visual way to assess peptide quality after receiving?
Visual inspection is a basic but not reliable quality check. A good nasal spray should be clear to slightly opalescent, colourless or very pale yellow, with no visible particulates. Cloudiness, visible particles, precipitate, or off-colours indicate degradation or contamination. These are minimum filters only — many forms of peptide degradation or contamination are invisible. The COA is the irreplaceable primary quality document.
How long before research effects are observable?
This varies by compound and endpoint. Semax and Selank show subjective effects in some researchers within 15–30 minutes of a first dose. PT-141 effects typically onset at 30–90 minutes. BPC-157’s repair-related outcomes require weeks of consistent administration. DSIP sleep effects are observed acutely on the night of administration for most subjects. Oxytocin’s social cognition effects are dose-dependent and context-dependent, peaking at 45–75 minutes.
Where can I read the primary literature for these compounds?
PubMed (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov) is the primary source. Search compound name + mechanism of interest. For Semax and Selank, the majority of peer-reviewed literature is in Russian-language journals indexed in PubMed — use the English-language abstracts. For BPC-157, Professor Sikirić’s group at the University of Zagreb has published extensively. For oxytocin, search the work of Knut Birnbaumer, Larry Young, and the Zak/Fehr groups for human social cognition studies.
