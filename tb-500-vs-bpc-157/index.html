TB-500 vs BPC-157: long chain vs 15-mer.

TB-500 and BPC-157 are the two most cross-searched repair-research peptides, so buyers compare them constantly — but they aren't the same kind of molecule. BPC-157 is a short, single-sequence synthetic pentadecapeptide of 15 amino acids; TB-500 is derived from thymosin beta-4, a much longer chain, and is sold as either the full sequence or a short fragment of it. That size difference — and the molecular-weight check it forces on the TB-500 COA — is the whole comparison. This page lays out what each is, the documentation standard behind both, and what to confirm before sourcing either.

A long chain vs a short 15-mer

The structural distinction researchers care about: BPC-157 is a short synthetic pentadecapeptide — 15 amino acids in one defined sequence — while TB-500 is derived from thymosin beta-4, a much longer chain, and is sold as either the full ~43-residue protein or only its short actin-binding fragment. They are not the same size of molecule and are not interchangeable. Titan handles both strictly as research compounds, never as substitutes for one another or for any approved medication.

On TB-500, read the molecular weight first

The single biggest sourcing trap in this pair is that “TB-500” is a trade name used for two different molecules: the complete thymosin beta-4 sequence, and a shorter truncated fragment of it. Those carry very different masses, so the mass on the release sheet — not the label — is what tells you which one you actually received. Confirm the reported molecular weight against the form the supplier claims to ship before trusting anything else on the page.

View TB-500→

Same documentation standard for both

Whichever compound you're sourcing, the paper-trail standard does not change: a lot-matched in-house release sheet referenced to the lot code on the unit, against an HPLC purity target with identity confirmation by mass. For BPC-157 that mass is a single expected 15-mer value; for TB-500 it resolves the full-length-vs-fragment question. That consistency is what separates a documented supplier from a blind one — and it is the only claim Titan makes about these compounds.

Choose by the sequence your model needs

Pick by what your research model is built around: a short, single-sequence pentadecapeptide points to BPC-157; the thymosin beta-4 actin-binding region points to TB-500 — and there, confirm whether your protocol expects the full chain or the fragment. Avoid any supplier that attaches healing, injury-recovery, dosing, or human-use claims to either; that marketing language is a compliance red flag, not science.
